% -- Introduction --
\begin{tcolorbox}[glassy]
\sectitle{Introduction}

Solar flares are among the most energetic events in our solar system,
releasing enormous quantities of energy across the electromagnetic
spectrum in timescales ranging from milliseconds to hours. The underlying
physical mechanisms --- particularly sub-second oscillatory phenomena
known as \textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{Quasi-Periodic Pulsations (QPPs)}}
--- remain poorly understood because existing solar monitors lack the
temporal resolution and multi-band simultaneity needed to resolve them.

QPPs have been detected in microwave, hard X-ray, and EUV emissions,
but the temporal relationships \textit{between} these bands at sub-second
cadence are largely unconstrained. The spatial dynamics of the soft X-ray
source during a flare --- and the thermal evolution of the flare late
phase on centi-second timescales --- are similarly unexplored
observational frontiers.

The \textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{Active Solar Irradiance Observer (ASIO)}}
is designed specifically to fill these gaps. Scheduled for launch in 2027
aboard NASA's \textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{MUSE (MUlti-slit Solar
Explorer)}} spacecraft, ASIO delivers simultaneous high-cadence irradiance
measurements in the SXR and HXR bands with integrated coarse source
localization --- a capability not available in any existing solar instrument.
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{0.3cm}

% -- Research Objectives --
\begin{tcolorbox}[glassy]
\sectitle{Research Objectives}

\vspace{2pt}
\noindent\rqnum{1}\hspace{5pt}%
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.88\linewidth}
  Do sub-second \textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{Quasi-Periodic Pulsations
  (QPPs)}} exist simultaneously across the SXR, HXR, and EUV energy
  bands? Are there measurable temporal offsets between bands that reveal
  the causal sequence of energy release?
\end{minipage}

\vspace{5pt}
\noindent\rqnum{2}\hspace{5pt}%
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.88\linewidth}
  Are \textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{soft X-ray sources}} during solar
  flares spatially stationary, or do they exhibit rapid motion across the
  solar disk? If dynamic, over what spatial extent and on what timescale?
\end{minipage}

\vspace{5pt}
\noindent\rqnum{3}\hspace{5pt}%
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.88\linewidth}
  How does the \textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{thermal structure}} of a
  solar flare evolve during its late phase on centi-second timescales ---
  and what temperature and emission measure trajectories emerge as the
  plasma cools?
\end{minipage}

% -- The Instrument --
\begin{tcolorbox}[glassy]
\sectitle{The Instrument}

ASIO is a compact photometer engineered as a hosted payload on the
\textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{NASA MUSE spacecraft}}, which will occupy
a Sun-synchronous low Earth orbit at $\sim$667~km. The instrument leverages
MUSE's spacecraft bus for power, thermal control, and telemetry while
providing a high-cadence X-ray measurement channel fully independent of
MUSE's primary UV spectrograph.

The flight chassis is precision-machined from aluminum with multiple
aperture channels, each coupling solar X-rays to dedicated solid-state
photodiode detectors. A \textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{shadow plate}}
upstream of the SXR detector array provides coarse source localization
without imaging optics --- differential shadow patterns across detector
segments encode the angular position of the X-ray source on the solar
disk. An adjustable mounting ring permits fine alignment of the instrument
axis after spacecraft integration.

% -- Alignment Procedure --
\begin{tcolorbox}[glassy]
\sectitle{Alignment Procedure}

\noindent
\begin{minipage}[t]{0.62\linewidth}
  The shadow plate upstream of the SXR photodiode array encodes the
  angular position of a solar X-ray source as a differential shadow
  pattern. Comparing flux ratios between detector segments yields a
  coarse source centroid without imaging optics.
  Post-integration alignment is characterised with an automated
  edge-detection pipeline: exterior and interior aperture corners are
  located to sub-pixel accuracy, compared against the optical model,
  and the adjustable ring is locked once verified.
\end{minipage}\hfill%

% -- Instrument Testing --
\begin{tcolorbox}[glassy]
\sectitle{Instrument Testing}

All ASIO assembly and integration was performed under
\textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{Class~100 cleanroom conditions}} at MSU's
SSEL facility. Calibrated red laser sources illuminated the aperture
assembly on an optical bench to map the spatial response of each
photodiode channel and validate the shadow-plate positional sensing
concept. Differential illumination patterns matched ray-trace model
predictions, confirming readiness for flight integration.

% -- Data Analysis --
\begin{tcolorbox}[glassy]
\sectitle{Data Analysis}

ASIO's science pipeline converts multi-band irradiance time series into
\textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{temperature}} and
\textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{emission measure}} using ratios of SXR and
HXR fluxes under an isothermal plasma assumption. Quasi-Periodic Pulsations
are identified via \textbf{\textcolor{accentdark}{continuous wavelet
decomposition}}, which resolves the time-frequency structure of the
irradiance signal and isolates statistically significant periodic components
above global (95\%) and local (99\%) significance thresholds. Synthetic
signal validation confirms robust QPP recovery from realistic noise
environments prior to first-light solar observations.
